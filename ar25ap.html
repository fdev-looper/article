Phish and Swish: Tricks That Make You Click in a Flick

From AI-personalized emails to SVG-in-PDFs and QR-code scams, phishing has grown smarter and harder to spot. Here's what everyone should know.

Cybersecurity
October 2025
6 min read

When Clicking Becomes Dangerous

Phishing can target anyone. Recently, I ordered from a popular e-commerce site. The website confirmed that my payment went through and the item had shipped, but I also got an email saying the payment failed and asking me to retry. The email looked real, with logo, tone, even a support footer, and pushed me to click a link. That confusion is exactly what scammers exploit. Checking the official website directly saved me from handing over anything sensitive.

This isn't an isolated incident. In my previous articles (Article 12 on QR phishing, Article 3 on OTP scams, and Article 4 on Quishing attacks), I've explored how these threats continue to evolve. Each method shares a common thread: exploiting trust and urgency to bypass our natural skepticism. Understanding these interconnected tactics is crucial for staying protected in today's digital landscape.

Quishing: QR Code Phishing

Scammers hide malicious links in QR codes inside emails, PDFs, posters, or chat messages. A quick scan lands victims on spoofed sites, bypassing the usual URL inspection. Cofense and Trend Micro report that quishing campaigns are rising rapidly and often target students and online shoppers who use QR codes for convenience.

QR codes are everywhere now, in restaurant menus, payment systems, event tickets. This familiarity makes people scan without thinking. But unlike a regular link where you might notice something suspicious in the URL, QR codes hide the destination completely until after you've scanned.

Multi-Channel and Voice-Based Attacks

Attackers combine emails, chat apps, and phone calls. Sometimes a cloned voice or AI-generated message is used to build trust and urgency. Check Point and Microsoft warn that these cross-platform attacks can fool even careful users by appearing familiar and authoritative.

Picture this: you get an email from your bank, then immediately receive a text message, followed by a phone call from someone who sounds exactly like your bank representative. Each channel reinforces the others, making the scam feel legitimate. The voice might even be AI-generated to sound like someone you know or a trusted institution.

Data from Kaspersky shows hundreds of millions of phishing attempts blocked in 2024, while Cofense reports high rates of credential theft from QR and multi-channel campaigns. Targeted spearphishing has a much higher success rate than generic emails, proving these advanced methods are profitable for attackers.

Why These Tactics Work So Well

Scammers exploit urgency, authority, curiosity, and habit. A message asking for a "quick code confirmation" or an invoice review seems routine, which lowers suspicion. Adding personal details from social media makes these attacks even more convincing.

Think about how you interact online daily. You get dozens of emails, multiple notifications, constant requests for action. When something seems urgent and familiar, your brain takes shortcuts. Scammers know this and design their attacks to trigger these automatic responses before your critical thinking kicks in.

Protecting Yourself: Practical Steps

Always hover to check links. Before clicking, pause and hover your mouse over any link to see the actual URL. If it looks suspicious or doesn't match the supposed sender, don't click.

Verify sender addresses manually. Check the email address carefully. Scammers use addresses that look almost right but have small differences like extra letters or different domains.

Treat unexpected attachments with caution. Don't open attachments from unknown senders. Even if you recognize the sender, be suspicious of unexpected files, especially PDFs with interactive content.

Never enter credentials from a link. If you get an email asking you to log in, don't click the link. Open a new browser tab and go directly to the official website to log in.

Never share OTPs with anyone. No legitimate company will ever call or message asking for your one-time password or verification code. If someone asks for it, they're a scammer.

Enable multi-factor authentication. Use an authenticator app like Google Authenticator or Authy. Even if someone gets your password, they can't access your account without the second factor.

Keep everything updated. Update your apps, PDF viewers, browsers, and operating system regularly. Updates often patch security vulnerabilities that attackers exploit.

For Organizations: Building Defense Layers

Enforce SPF, DKIM, and DMARC. These email authentication protocols prevent domain spoofing and make it harder for attackers to impersonate your organization.

Use modern email filters. Invest in advanced threat protection that can detect AI-generated phishing, malicious PDFs, and suspicious patterns.

Require MFA for important systems. Make multi-factor authentication mandatory for email, financial systems, and any platform with sensitive data.

Run regular awareness exercises. Conduct phishing simulations and training. Reports from Proofpoint, Cofense, and Microsoft all recommend combining technical controls with regular training.

Quick Red Flag Checklist

Watch out for these warning signs:

Domain typos: Small differences in email addresses or URLs like "amaz0n.com" instead of "amazon.com"

Unexpected urgency: Messages creating panic or demanding immediate action

Attachments asking for credentials: PDFs or documents that want you to log in

QR codes in unusual places: Codes appearing in emails when you'd normally expect a regular link

Follow-ups on different platforms: Email followed immediately by text or call reinforcing the same message

PDFs with interactive content: Documents that redirect you to websites or ask for logins

If you suspect phishing, do not click links, verify with the official website or known contacts directly.

Stay One Step Ahead

Phishing is evolving fast, but simple habits remain powerful defenses. Pause before you click, verify carefully, and treat any urgent request for credentials or personal information with caution.

A single second of care, like the one that saved me from that fake e-commerce email, can prevent a lot of trouble. Start applying these practices today, and you'll significantly reduce your risk of becoming a victim.

Cybersecurity Dictionary
AI (Artificial Intelligence)
noun - AI stands for Artificial Intelligence. Technology that enables machines to mimic human intelligence, increasingly used by attackers to create convincing phishing messages and cloned voices.
Credential Theft
noun - The act of stealing usernames, passwords, or other login information through deceptive means such as phishing or social engineering.
DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)
noun - DKIM stands for DomainKeys Identified Mail. An email authentication method that allows the receiver to verify that an email was actually sent by the domain it claims to be from.
DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication)
noun - DMARC stands for Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting, and Conformance. An email protocol that works with SPF and DKIM to prevent email spoofing.
MFA (Multi-Factor Authentication)
noun - MFA stands for Multi-Factor Authentication. A security system that requires two or more verification methods to access an account, making unauthorized access significantly harder.
OTP (One-Time Password)
noun - OTP stands for One-Time Password. A temporary verification code sent to your device that expires after a single use or short time period, used for secure login authentication.
PDF (Portable Document Format)
noun - PDF stands for Portable Document Format. A universal file format developed by Adobe that can contain text, images, forms, and sometimes embedded malicious content or interactive elements.
Phishing
noun - A cybercrime technique where attackers use disguised emails, texts, or websites to trick victims into revealing personal information, passwords, or financial details.
QR (Quick Response)
noun - QR stands for Quick Response. A two-dimensional barcode that can be scanned with smartphones to quickly access websites, information, or payments. Often exploited in quishing attacks.
Quishing
noun - A portmanteau of "QR" and "phishing." A cyber attack where scammers hide malicious links inside QR codes to bypass traditional URL inspection and security filters.
Spearphishing
noun - A highly targeted phishing attack aimed at specific individuals or organizations, often using personalized information to appear more legitimate and trustworthy.
SPF (Sender Policy Framework)
noun - SPF stands for Sender Policy Framework. An email authentication protocol that prevents spammers from sending messages on behalf of your domain by verifying sender IP addresses.
SVG (Scalable Vector Graphics)
noun - SVG stands for Scalable Vector Graphics. An XML-based image format that can contain embedded scripts or code, sometimes exploited by attackers to hide malicious content within PDFs.
URL (Uniform Resource Locator)
noun - URL stands for Uniform Resource Locator. The web address you type or click to access websites. Attackers often create URLs that look similar to legitimate ones to deceive victims.
Vishing (Voice Phishing)
noun - A combination of "voice" and "phishing." A social engineering attack conducted via phone calls where scammers impersonate legitimate organizations to steal sensitive information.
