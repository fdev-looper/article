Gen Z faces unique challenges: higher anxiety, social media pressure, economic uncertainty, and the burden of making "right" choices in an increasingly complex world.

Ever had a fight with your sibling where both of you were 100% sure the other was wrong? That moment when emotions run high and even a small argument feels huge? This is a small example of how tricky it can be to figure out what's truly right or wrong. Everyone sees the same situation differently, and understanding that difference is the first step in deciding what's right.

Different Perspectives Shape Reality

🚬 The Smoking Dilemma

Smoking is often seen as wrong because it harms health. But the smoker might think: "Am I hurting anyone else? I know the risks, but it's my choice." Both perspectives are valid in their own context.

🗳️ Political Views

Two people can look at the same political event and form opposite opinions. One sees a policy as fair, another as harmful. Upbringing and exposure shape how we see things.

🕊️ Religious Beliefs

Religious or spiritual beliefs guide many choices. Certain rituals or moral codes may define right or wrong differently for different people.

💝 Relationships

In personal relationships, deciding right or wrong can be tricky. Suppose a friend borrows money and can't return it on time. You might feel it's wrong, but from their perspective, they are doing their best under tough circumstances.

How We Actually Decide

Personal Values: Our own values — honesty, fairness, kindness — guide decisions. A white lie may feel okay to you but completely wrong to someone else.

Culture and Upbringing: Family, friends, and society shape how we see right and wrong. Some actions are normal in one culture but frowned upon in another.

Experience vs Self-Created Values: Many clashes happen because people create their own values without enough life experience. Creating your own principles is good, but someone who hasn't spent decades observing life may not fully understand consequences. This is where learning from elders or experienced people becomes important.

A Simple Rule for Right and Wrong

Choices that don't harm humanity, don't destroy your happiness, and don't hurt your loved ones' happiness are usually right.

Other points to consider:

  • Intent matters: Good intentions often indicate a morally right approach.
  • Empathy counts: Think about how your action affects others emotionally and practically.
  • Long-term consequences: Short-term pleasure may create long-term harm.
  • See through others' eyes: If a decision feels wrong, try to view it from the other person's perspective.

The Final Perspective

Right and wrong are mostly about perspective.

Before making decisions, pause and think about your values, reasoning, and the bigger picture. Emotions matter, but don't let them control your choices blindly. Take a moment to reflect, consider other perspectives, and balance your judgment with experience and filtered ancient wisdom — that's how we can make better, conscious decisions.
